# Stock Market Foundations

## 1. What is a Stock?
A stock (or share) represents **fractional ownership** in a corporation. When you buy a share, you are buying a piece of that company's future earnings and assets.

## 2. How the Market Works
*   **The Exchange:** Think of this as a digital flea market (e.g., NYSE, NASDAQ) where buyers and sellers meet.
*   **The Broker:** Since you can't walk onto the floor of the NYSE, you use a broker (e.g., Fidelity, Schwab, Robinhood) to execute trades for you.
*   **The Price:** Driven by **Supply and Demand**. If more people want to buy a stock than sell it, the price goes up.

## 3. Key Terminology
*   **Market Cap:** The total value of the company (Share Price x Total Shares).
    *   *Large-cap:* >$10B (Stable, e.g., Apple)
    *   *Small-cap:* <$2B (High growth, high risk)
*   **Bull Market:** When prices are rising and optimism is high.
*   **Bear Market:** When prices are falling (usually 20% or more from recent highs) and pessimism prevails.
*   **Dividends:** A portion of company profits paid out to shareholders (usually by established, "Value" companies).

## 4. How You Make Money
1.  **Capital Gains:** Buying low and selling high.
2.  **Dividends:** Passive income paid out while you hold the stock.

## 5. First Steps for Beginners
*   **Start with an Index Fund (ETF):** Instead of picking one company, buy a "basket" like the S&P 500 (ticker: VOO or SPY). It tracks the 500 largest US companies, giving you instant diversification.
*   **Understand Risk:** Never invest money you need for rent or bills next month. The market is volatile in the short term but historically trends upward in the long term.

## 6. Fundamental Analysis (The "Health" Check)
This involves looking at a company's financial state to determine its "intrinsic value."
*   **P/E Ratio (Price-to-Earnings):** Helps you see if a stock is expensive or cheap relative to its profit.
*   **Revenue vs. Net Income:** Revenue is total sales; Net Income is what’s left after expenses (actual profit).
*   **Debt-to-Equity:** Shows if the company is funded by its own money or borrowed cash.

## 7. Technical Analysis (The "Chart" Check)
This involves studying price patterns and volume to predict future movements.
*   **Support & Resistance:** "Floors" where price usually stops falling and "Ceilings" where it stops rising.
*   **Moving Averages (MA):** Smoothes out price data to show the trend direction (e.g., 50-day or 200-day MA).
*   **RSI (Relative Strength Index):** A momentum indicator that shows if a stock is "Overbought" (too expensive) or "Oversold" (potentially a bargain).
